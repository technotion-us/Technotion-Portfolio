Community Energy Futures: Using IxD to Study Social Dynamics in Microgrids

Class: UT330, Interaction Design & Urban Experiences

Instructor: Matthew Wizinsky

Skills: UX Research, Interaction Design, Speculative Design, Service Design

TLDR:

Designed multiple interaction design prototypes representing different ways residents could share energy with eachother. Conducted UX Research through multiple generative and exploratory methods; developed personas and user journey map with collected info. Through iterative user testing, documented how each change affected behavior and overall social outcomes. Through comprehension testing, recorded how users percieved different design choices.

Setting the Stage

The transition to sustainable energy is one of the paradigmatic challenges of the 21st century, unfolding at many scales through social, technical, legal, and economic changes. In 2024, residents of Ann Arbor, MI (USA), voted to create a community-owned Sustainable Energy Utility (SEU), an opt-in energy utility providing 100% renewable energy from local solar and battery storage systems at participating homes and businesses. These efforts indicate new possibilities for community investment and energy management. However, the transition from a corporate utility to networked “microgrids” is not without friction.

Shifting from a corporate utility to networked “microgrids” brings opportunities but also new social friction. How will neighbors and local businesses work together to form new coalitions for energy production and sharing? Who will decide what counts as “surplus” energy and what should be done with it? During outages, how will microgrid participants choose whether to sell, share, or trade energy?

The project aspires to anticipate and inform policy on the SEU that will shape new social interactions in the city. By offering multiple possible forms of interaction scenarios, the project makes the SEU tangible as a new terrain for both conflict and collaboration. This project explores these questions through interaction design prototypes that allow residents to experience different microgrid scenarios. The project helps communities express preferences for different forms of energy cooperation, inform SEU policy, highlight possible social tensions, and make the SEU tangible as a space for both conflict and collaboration.

Service Design Mindset

Energy production and sharing in microgrids can create social friction, as neighbors experience new transactional relationships. Here, we propose multiple UX/UI prototypes, and identify potential value tensions throughout the service journey and user flow.
